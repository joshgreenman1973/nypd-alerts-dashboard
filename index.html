Read the homicide count with care

The feed carries — alerts labeled "Homicide," but that is not a murder count. Only — carry a 2026 case number; — are follow-up updates (victim IDs, arrests, added media) re-advising incidents already in the feed, and the "Homicide" label is the department's initial framing — it includes deaths later ruled non-criminal. Reading it as a body count would contradict the NYPD's own July 2 announcement of record-low murders for the first half of 2026. Every offense label here is a press-office subject line, not a verified crime classification.

How this was built

  • Source. Every email from the NYPD Deputy Commissioner of Public Information (DCPI.DCPI@nypd.org) piped into a Slack channel, Jan 1 through Jul 4 2026. One message per email.
  • What is counted. Each row is one press message. "Crime advisories" are per-incident alerts; "original incidents" strip out follow-up updates. Fields (precinct, offense, case number, update/arrest/pattern flags) were parsed from each email's subject line.
  • Completeness check. The NYPD's own sequential case numbers (RMA) run contiguously from 1 to 1,653 across the period with no gaps, confirming the full year-to-date series was captured. One out-of-range number (1982) is a source typo, kept verbatim.
  • Boroughs are inferred from precinct number (1–34 Manhattan, 40–52 Bronx, 60–94 Brooklyn, 100–115 Queens, 120–123 Staten Island); transit and housing commands are shown separately.
  • Limitations. Labels are the department's, not independently verified. Arrest follow-through reflects press behavior, not case clearances. No arrestee or victim names are included in this dataset.
Everything substantive here — the concept, the decision to structure the NYPD press feed, and the framing of every chart — was handled by a human author. The coding, parsing and file work were primarily in the hands of Anthropic's Claude and Claude Code, under close direction. Quality-control measures actually performed on this project: the department's own sequential case numbers were cross-checked for gaps to confirm the year-to-date series is complete (1 through 1,653, contiguous); the homicide figure was interrogated against the NYPD's record-low-murders announcement and is flagged rather than presented as a body count; offense and borough groupings were derived by transparent rule from the raw subject lines; and no personal identifying information was carried into the dataset. Nevertheless, the risks of using AI remain — labels are the department's own and were not independently verified — and we would be especially grateful to learn of any red flags you notice.
